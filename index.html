Have you had an accident at work?



In 2016 there were 2.9 million non-fatal workplace injuries and illnesses reported by private industry employers, a rate of 2.9 cases per 100 full-time employees. 892,270 of those reported resulted in days off work - on average, 8 - This probably won't surpise you, as it's a situation many people have been in.
An unwelcome shock however is the fact that in the same year a total of 5,190 people lost their lives at work in the USA. The Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) highlighted that it was the third consecutive increase in annual workplace fatalities, and the first time more than 5,000 fatalities have been recorded by the Census of Fatal Occupational Injuries (CFOI) since 2008.
So who are these workers, and what's causing these accidents? And is the risk of the job worth the reward?

Generally, industries with a high injury rate have a high workplace fatality rate - but those occupations with a high fatality rate may not correspond to a high injury rate.

Workers in occupations involving manual labouring - such as Farming, Construction or Transpotation - have accident rates far above the national average.

The cause of injury in accidents also varies significantly depending on the overall severity of the accident. Transportation Incidents accounted for well over a third of deaths at work in 2016 at 2,083 cases, but were much less often the cause of a non-fatal injury. Conversely, Overexertion and Bodily Reaction - resulting in strains, sprains and tears - was the main culprit of injuries in the workplace, but in no cases led to a death.

Deaths due to Violence by Person or Animals increased upon the previous year, with workplace homicides up by 83 cases to 500, and workplace suicdes up by 62 cases to 291. The BLS reports that this is the highest homicide figure since 2010, and the most suicides since the CFOI began reporting data in 1992.
